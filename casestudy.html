CASE STUDY

Solar-powered & temperature-controlled model building

Goal & Motivation:

Greenhouse gas emissions have produced widespread, irreversible effects on the environment that will continue to grow. Converting to renewable energy sources, such as solar power, can address this crisis. Wind and solar power alone can generate 100% of the average energy demand in the US, significantly reducing the global impact of fossil fuel consumption.

The primary motivation behind this project is to design and test a solar-powered model building to demonstrate the feasibility of utilizing solar power to regulate temperature.

Constraints:

  • Maintain a comfortable indoor temperature of 20-25 °C during a cool sunny day (φ>750 W/m2, T 9-19 °C)
  • Store thermal energy to maintain temperature for 30 minutes of cloud cover
  • Record all relevant temperatures (inside, outside, & thermal mass) and display data on LCD screen
  • 100% solar-powered

Step 1: Initial design

  • Heat energy enters the system through one large window
  • One solar panels on the roof
  • Two ventilation flaps on opposite sides to maximize air flow between them (controlled by 180° servo motor)
  • Shading mechanism over the window (controlled by 360 servo motor)

Step 2: Initial calculations

Total surface area = 0.46 m2

Thermal resistance Rth = 0.38 W/m2

Power loss Qout = 22.4 W (assuming ∆T = 8.5 °C)

Solar vector (in Davidson at noon in December) = 32.5°

Window area = 0.03 m2 (assuming 22.4 W power, 1000 W/m2 solar flux, 0.9 transmission, and 32.5° solar angle)

Step 3: Print and assemble model

Using Adobe Illustrator, laser cutter, and matboard

Step 4: Indoor experiment

Methods:

  • Heated model with 3 lightbulbs (7 W each) for 20 mins, then cooled for 20 mins
  • Collected data with temperature sensors inside and outside on SD card
  • Graphed a logarithmic function of Tin with a linear fit to find Tmin and Tmax values

Results:

  • Tmin = 23.75°C, Tmax = 29°C
  • ∆T = 5.25°C, Rth = 0.25°C/W

Discussion:

  • 38% error for ∆T and 34% for Rth
  • Likely due to gaps created by inserting temperature sensors and lightbulbs, allowing a significant amount of heat to escape

Step 5: Outdoor experiment

Methods:

  • Same as experiment 1 using solar power & manually applying cooling mechanisms

Results:

  • Tmin = 18°C, Tmax = 26°C
  • ∆T = 8°C, Rth = 0.35°C/W

Discussion:

  • 6% error for ∆T and 8% for Rth, showing substantial improvement

Step 6: Thermal capacity experiment

Methods

  • Recorded temperatures with water thermal capacity (1848 J/°C)

Results

  • Indoor temperature successfully remained constant after outside temperature dropped

Added ceramic container with 464 J/°C for a total thermal capacity of 2312 J/°C

Step 7: Print and assemble final version

Changes

  • Switched direction of top flap to increase air flow
  • Added layer of fabric insulation to roof to prevent heat loss

Step 12: Analysis

Results:

  • Heating and cooling mechanisms were effective
  • Thermal capacity temperature remained relatively constant at 17.5-18℃
  • Indoor temp stayed within 20-25℃ range for the majority of the three-hour experimental period
  • High temps from 0-4000 secs - shade system was not functioning correctly due to switched sensors
  • Sharp drop around 7000 seconds - aligns with decrease in solar flux when the sun went behind a cloud

Discussion:

  • Sources of error - didn't account for cooling due to wind, gap from inserting temp sensors through ventilation flap
  • Improvements - increase thermal capacity (maintain temp for longer period with low solar flux), reduce threshold range for cooling mechanisms (activate more often, minimizing large temp fluctuations)

Applications & feasibility:

  • Model was energy efficient and cooling mechanisms were effective - could be applied on larger scale, but would be difficult to rely entirely on solar power (requires continuous high solar flux)

Takeaways

  • Gained experience with the engineering design process - continuously making improvements, collecting data to inform changes
  • Integrated different disciplines (physics, math, computer science, environmental science, & art) to create a project with tangible applications
  • Collaborated in a team with diverse perspectives and levels of experience across different topics
  • Strengthened understanding of thermodynamics, solar energy, energy storage, & electronics principles and their applications
  • Technical skills - building Arduino circuits, C++ programming, data visualization
